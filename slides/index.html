What marketing is really for

Not pretty pictures.
Real people, becoming customers.

A founder I know raised money, hired an expensive agency, built a beautiful brand — logo, website, colors — all based on what HE liked, not what customers needed. He spent a lot, felt he had to keep it, but customers didn't connect with it. Nothing sold. The lesson — the most expensive one in marketing: marketing is NOT pretty pictures or the launch-day celebration. Its one job is to bring real people in and turn them into customers. Content with no strategy is just noise. Buying ads before you understand your customer is spending money to be ignored faster.

You can't be the answer
if you don't know the question.

Go slow — to go fast.

The first real rule. You can't be the answer if you don't know the question. Who is your customer? What do they need? What keeps them awake at night? Know this BEFORE you make anything. In English we say: go slow, to go fast. Time on the foundation first makes everything after it faster and cheaper.

The map: your customer's journey

Awareness→ Acquisition→ Adoption→ Advocacy
Picture your customer on a journey of four stages. AWARENESS: they don't know you exist; their question is "does a solution like this exist?" — your content says here I am, here's the problem I solve. ACQUISITION: they're comparing options; question is "why choose you?" Don't fear it — it's like math, no emotion, yes or no, don't take it personally. I'd rather they read my content and say "not for me" than say yes and be wrong. ADOPTION: they paid — are they actually USING it? The stage everyone forgets. (You've all signed up for something, never used it, and hit "cancel" a year later.) Your content answers "how do I get the most out of this?" ADVOCACY: they love it and want to tell people — help them. After a great result I ask a client for a 5-minute video: what was hard, what we did, how it is now. That becomes content that brings the next customer.

Different stage.
Different question.
Different answer.

Here's why it matters: most people make ONE kind of content and wonder why it fails. But each stage is a different question — and your content is the answer. Four questions, four answers. Match your content to the stage your customer is in. That's the entire strategy. That's what it means to be the answer.
