What marketing is really for

Not pretty pictures.
Real people, becoming customers.

~43% of startups fail from poor product–market fit — the #1 root cause, ahead of anything tactical. CB Insights · startup post-mortems

A founder I know raised money, hired an expensive agency, built a beautiful brand — logo, website, colors — all based on what HE liked, not what customers needed. He spent a lot, felt he had to keep it, but customers didn't connect with it. Nothing sold. The lesson — the most expensive one in marketing: marketing is NOT pretty pictures or the launch-day celebration. Its one job is to bring real people in and turn them into customers. Content with no strategy is just noise. Buying ads before you understand your customer is spending money to be ignored faster.

The first real rule. You can't be the answer if you don't know the question. Who is your customer? What do they need? What keeps them awake at night? Know this BEFORE you make anything. In English we say: go slow, to go fast. Time on the foundation first makes everything after it faster and cheaper.

The map: your customer's journey

Awareness→ Acquisition→ Adoption→ Advocacy

Four stages. Each one a different question —
and a different place you capture value.

Now the most useful idea in the talk. Picture your customer on a journey of four stages. I'll walk each one — and at each stage, notice WHERE value gets captured, because that's what you're really building toward. Keep it simple: four stages, four questions, four answers.

The customer's journey · stage 1 of 4

Awareness

"Does a solution like this
even exist?"

Your content's job: be seen — name the problem you solve.

Value captured → you exist to them
AWARENESS. Right now your customer doesn't know you exist. The first job is simply to be seen. Their question: "does a solution like this even exist?" Your content answers it — here I am, here's the problem I solve. The value you capture: you go from invisible to existing. Before this moment you were nothing to them. After it, you're an option.

The customer's journey · stage 2 of 4

Acquisition

"Why should I
choose you?"

Your content's job: help them decide — honestly.

Value captured → they choose you
ACQUISITION. Now they know you exist, but they're comparing options. Their question: "why should I choose you?" Many founders fear this — they think it means selling, and "I'm bad at selling." Reframe it: acquisition is like math. No emotion. Yes or no. Don't take it personally. Your job is to give them what they need to decide — and honestly, I'd rather they read my content and say "not for me" than say yes and be the wrong fit. Value captured: a customer — and the right one.

The customer's journey · stage 3 of 4

Adoption

"How do I get the most
out of this?"

Your content's job: help them actually succeed.

Value captured → they stay
ADOPTION — the stage everyone forgets. They paid. But are they actually USING it? You've all done this: you sign up for something, never use it, and a year later an email says "renew?" and you think cancel, cancel, cancel. That's a failure of adoption. Your content answers "how do I get the most out of this?" Value captured: they stay — the renewal, the habit, the retained customer. This is where profit actually lives.

The customer's journey · stage 4 of 4

Advocacy

"How do I
tell others?"

Your content's job: make sharing easy.

Value captured → they bring the next customer
ADVOCACY. Now they love it — and a happy customer wants to tell people. When they do, they become a salesforce you didn't pay for. But they need your help. My favorite move: after a great result, I ask the client for a short five-minute video — what was hard before, what we did, how it is now. That video becomes content that brings the next customer. Value captured: your customer starts acquiring your next customer for you. That's the flywheel.

Different stage.
Different question.
Different answer.

Here's why it matters: most people make ONE kind of content and wonder why it fails. But each stage is a different question — and your content is the answer. Four questions, four answers. Match your content to the stage your customer is in. That's the entire strategy. That's what it means to be the answer.
